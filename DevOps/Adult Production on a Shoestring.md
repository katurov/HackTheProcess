# Adult Production on a Shoestring

This is a comprehensive breakdown of my talk prepared for NSTechTalk. My goal was to demonstrate that modern DevOps practices are no longer a burden for the solo developer—they are easily packaged into the operational logic of an AI agent.

On my own server, I use a Gemini-CLI agent that acts as a tireless Junior DevOps. It investigates system failures, installs software, and provisions dedicated virtual machines in Proxmox on my behalf. It operates based on a lean set of core rules, keeping things simple and efficient. However, if you truly want to "overclock" your agent's capabilities, I highly recommend feeding it [this repository](https://github.com/AnastasiyaW/claude-code-config) — it is more than capable of "borrowing" these skills and integrating them into your workflow.

A crucial word on safety: If you choose to run Gemini with root privileges, you **must** either verify every single decision it makes (and force it to explain its reasoning) or be prepared to rely on luck worthy of a Spanish Conquistador. Personally, I run it as root — and I don't find it "insane" at all; I find it liberating.

Below is the full breakdown of the presentation, mapping the narrative vision against the concrete technical steps to do.

## How to keep your pet project out of the project cemetry by Managing a Lab via AI

| Main Slide Content (The "Narrative") | Technical Action Items / Step-by-Step |
| --- | --- |
| **Slide 1: DevOps for Pet Projects**<br><br>We all love starting something new: that very "drive" of the first line of code, when the idea feels genius. But often our hobby turns into a burden or, even worse, into a "cemetery of abandoned scripts" that we are terrified to run a month later. Today, I want to show you the concept of **"adult production on a shoestring."** This is an approach that allows a solo engineer to preserve that very spark and the pleasure of knowing their project actually works and brings value, rather than just taking up disk space.<br><br><br>At the heart of my story lies personal experience of transitioning from chaotic edits to a stable system. I traveled this entire path using simple but reliable tools, seasoned with the power of artificial intelligence. I promise that today we won't talk about the pain of being on-call at night or complex corporate dashboards. We will talk about how to make DevOps your invisible ally. | **Technical Start:**<br><br>1. Clearly formulate a minimum viable goal (MVP) and do not bloat it.<br><br>2. Choose a stack you know best, without trying to learn five new technologies at once.<br><br>3. Automate the very first deploy, even if the project has only one button: the path to the server must be paved immediately. |
| **Slide 2: The Tragedy of the "Pet Cemetery"**<br><br>Why do our ideas die? Usually, a pet project lives exactly as long as the developer's eyes are glowing. But as soon as deployment starts taking more than five minutes or everything "crumbles" after a server reboot—we give up. The local address `http://localhost:3000/` is a cozy cradle, but the world beyond its borders scares us with the complexity of server settings, consoles, and potential bills from cloud giants like AWS.<br><br><br>The problem isn't that we are bad programmers, but that moving code to where it becomes available to users requires a completely different expertise. This "inherent complexity" and the need to remember the server prevent us from developing the idea itself. My task today is to show how to make it so that modern DevOps practices do not hinder you, but create a foundation on which a project can grow without your minute-by-minute control. | **Technical Advice:**<br><br>1. Limit the time for "researching" new tools—no more than 2 hours for choosing a database or framework.<br><br>2. Use `docker-compose` locally from day one: this guarantees that the development environment will be identical to the environment on the server.<br><br>3. Pre-determine the conditions for "freezing" a project so as not to waste resources on something that has stopped bringing pleasure. |
| **Slide 3: The Foundation: The Shared Core**<br><br>Even if you are working alone, you need an "adult" approach to infrastructure. This is not bureaucracy, but a way to save your time on context switching. Imagine your server or even your home computer as a small but real data center. Instead of raising a separate database for each small project, create one powerful instance of Postgres or Redis and separate projects by schemas or users.<br><br><br>This "ready-to-grow" approach accustoms your code to the right standards from day one. For example, using S3-compatible storage such as MinIO forces you to work with cloud patterns instead of a local file system. And rigid isolation through Proxmox or Docker guarantees that if one of your experiments "eats" all the memory or catches a critical bug, your other services won't even notice the problem. | **Technical Plan:**<br><br>1. Rent the cheapest cloud VPS for $5-10 for a quick start.<br><br>2. Install Proxmox or clean Docker there and deploy shared services: Postgres and MinIO.<br><br>3. As soon as the monthly cloud bill exceeds $20-30, buy a used mini-PC (NUC) and move everything to your home circuit—this will pay for itself in six months. |
| **Slide 4: Infrastructure as a Dialogue**<br><br>The main secret of simplicity in 2026 is turning your server into a conversation partner. I choose Proxmox not because it's fashionable, but because it is amazingly managed through text configs and the console. This is exactly the format that modern LLM agents handle best. You no longer configure the server manually for hours; you conduct a dialogue with your agent, who goes in via SSH and performs the routine for you.<br><br><br>This changes the paradigm itself: you operate with names and logic, while the agent handles the implementation. If you need to try a new technology, you simply ask the AI to create an isolated LXC container with the necessary parameters. This gives you smooth resource adjustment and that very "autonomy" that any developer dreams of. Your server becomes a flexible environment that adapts to your thoughts, rather than dictating its limitations. | **Setup Example (Illustration: Chat window with an agent):**<br><br>1. Create a separate SSH user on the server for the agent with limited rights.<br><br>2. Pass the IP address and access keys to the agent.<br><br>3. Give a clear instruction: "Write and apply a systemd config for my service in the folder `/opt/myapp` so that it automatically starts at system boot and writes logs to `journald`." |
| **Slide 5: Culture: Tools of Discipline**<br><br>Discipline is what allows you to forget about a project for six months and then come back and update it with one button. First rule: Git is the law. This is the only legal way to deliver code to the server. No edits via FTP or manual file copying. Git is not just transport, it is your backup and the history of your intentions. It is a checkpoint that separates the "test" zone and "production."<br><br><br>Second rule: secrets should never be in the code. Use `.env` files as law—this makes your project portable to any environment. I recommend starting trials in cloud Free Tiers where everything starts up quickly, and when the solution has "taken root"—taking it into your home circuit. Remember: deployment is a process that your agent should get used to, not you personally. Your task is only to point it to the right practices. | **Technical Hygiene:**<br><br>1. Create a Git repository for each project.<br><br>2. Set up the simplest CI/CD: either via GitHub Actions or via a regular `git pull` script on the server via a webhook.<br><br>3. Move all passwords and API keys to a `.env` file and make sure it is added to `.gitignore`. |
| **Slide 6: AI-agent — Your Junior DevOps**<br><br>The times when you had to be an expert in YAML configs and Dockerfiles are over. Now you have a "tireless Junior DevOps" who isn't too lazy to follow the rules and write endless settings for Nginx or Caddy. An agent is a subject to whom you delegate task execution, but it is important to do this consciously. Do not give it superuser rights (root), keep it within a dedicated virtual machine—this is your security level.<br><br><br>For an agent to be effective, it must "know" your current infrastructure map. I advise starting a separate document with a description of all connections and requiring the AI to update it. This will allow you to easily get a "second opinion" from different models. Remember that an agent is not a telepath: if you don't explain the system structure to it, it can get confused in the chain of reasoning just like an intern on their first working day. | **Technical Interaction:**<br><br>1. Use a standardized folder structure for all projects (for example, `/opt/projects/project_name`).<br><br>2. Always keep an `infra.md` file at the server root with a description of ports, volumes, and dependencies.<br><br>3. Give the agent context before the task: "I have Postgres in the `shared-db` container. Set up a separate schema and user for the new `site-v2` project without creating a new DB." |
| **Slide 7: "Data is Worth More Than Code"**<br><br>The most expensive mistake in DevOps is data loss. Code can always be re-downloaded from Git, but your databases and indices are unique. Therefore, your main prompt to the agent should always contain a requirement for persistence: "Keep in mind that data must be preserved when rebuilding the container." Train your AI to respect system paths like `/var/lib/data`, and this will save your money and time on recovery.<br><br><br>System reliability is built on the reuse of proven components. Instead of allowing the agent to spawn new databases for every sneeze, force it to use your Shared Core. Require the creation of new schemas and users in the existing infrastructure. This not only saves server resources but also makes your system predictable and easy to manage. | **Safety Engineering:**<br><br>1. Always specify volume mapping to specific host folders (for example, `./data:/var/lib/postgresql/data`) so that data does not "fly away" with the container.<br><br>2. Set up a daily backup of the data folder to S3/MinIO via a regular cron script.<br><br>3. Once a month, forcibly ask the agent to check the integrity of backups and restore data to a test container. |
| **Slide 8: Summary: Engineer Throughput**<br><br>In the end, we strive for one goal: "one-button production." Properly built DevOps for a pet project is not bureaucracy, but your personal freedom and autonomy. The project should work by itself while you sleep or come up with the next genius feature. When your ideas live in a stable and manageable ecosystem that costs pennies, you feel like not just a coder, but the owner of a working business.<br><br><br>Thanks to the agent, who takes on all the routine of operation, your throughput as an engineer grows 3–4 times. But the most important thing is that what you learn in the process becomes your most valuable asset. A working, updatable service is the best advertisement for your expertise. Don't be afraid of complexity, just delegate it to those who never tire of following the rules. | **Final Measurement:**<br><br>1. Measure your deployment time: if it takes more than one command in the terminal—this is your technical debt that needs to be automated.<br><br>2. Connect free monitoring (for example, UptimeRobot) to find out about a service crash before users do.<br><br>3. Organize your "Infrastructure as Code" (agent setting files) into a separate repository—this is your portfolio as a professional engineer. |

## P.S. The Death of the Solo Developer

During the Q&A session following the presentation, we stumbled upon an unexpected and somewhat striking realization. I started with the premise that *"Even if you are working alone, you need an 'adult' approach to infrastructure."* But by the end of the night, we realized the premise itself was outdated: **we are no longer working alone.**

Even if you are the only human in the room, you are now the lead of a synthetic team. You are managing a fleet of agents—some specialized in coding, others in infrastructure, and some in monitoring. This changes the role of DevOps entirely. Infrastructure is no longer just a place to host code; it is the **shared memory** and the **communication protocol** for your AI workforce.

The "adult approach" is now about ensuring seamless handovers between these agents. Managing how data flows within this synthetic team — and preserving the temporal context (the "when" and "why" of system changes) so that an agent six months from now understands the state of the project — has become a mission-critical task. We aren't just building systems for ourselves anymore; we are building environments where our agents can be successful.

The "Solo Engineer" has evolved into a "Synthetic Team Lead." And in this new reality, your infrastructure is the only thing keeping the team from falling into a hall-of-mirrors hallucination.
